# Accounting Rules

## Important Boundaries
- This is financial information, not professional advice â€” recommend CPA for complex tax situations
- Tax laws vary by jurisdiction and change frequently â€” verify current rules
- Audited financials require licensed accountants â€” internal tracking differs from official statements
- Material decisions need professional review â€” DIY works for basics, not for high stakes

## Core Principles
- Double-entry: every transaction has equal debit and credit â€” books must balance
- Accrual vs cash basis: accrual records when earned/owed, cash when money moves â€” pick one consistently
- Matching principle: record expenses in same period as related revenue â€” timing matters
- Conservatism: recognize losses immediately, gains only when realized â€” err toward understating
- Materiality: small errors don't matter, significant ones do â€” focus effort proportionally

## Financial Statements
- Balance sheet: assets = liabilities + equity at a point in time â€” snapshot of position
- Income statement: revenue - expenses = profit over a period â€” performance summary
- Cash flow statement: where cash came from and went â€” profitability doesn't mean liquidity
- These three are interconnected â€” changes in one affect others
- Read all three together â€” each tells part of the story

## Bookkeeping Basics
- Record transactions as they happen â€” catching up creates errors
- Keep receipts and documentation â€” proof matters for taxes and audits
- Reconcile bank accounts monthly â€” catch errors and fraud early
- Separate business and personal finances â€” mixing creates legal and tax problems
- Chart of accounts organizes categories â€” set up properly at the start

## Cash Flow Management
- Profit isn't cash â€” you can be profitable and run out of money
- Accounts receivable is money owed to you â€” track aging, follow up on late payments
- Accounts payable is money you owe â€” manage timing strategically
- Cash flow forecast: project inflows and outflows weekly â€” avoid surprises
- Keep cash reserves â€” 3-6 months of expenses minimum for stability

## Tax Fundamentals
- Track deductible expenses throughout year â€” reconstructing at tax time misses items
- Estimated taxes quarterly for self-employed â€” underpayment triggers penalties
- Depreciation spreads asset costs over useful life â€” immediate deduction vs long-term
- Understand the difference between tax avoidance (legal) and evasion (illegal)
- Deadlines are real â€” extensions to file aren't extensions to pay

## Common Mistakes
- Mixing personal and business accounts â€” destroys liability protection and complicates taxes
- Not tracking small cash expenses â€” they add up to significant deductions
- Ignoring accounts receivable aging â€” old invoices rarely get paid
- Forgetting to reconcile â€” errors compound when not caught early
- Waiting until year-end for tax planning â€” many strategies require advance action

## Key Ratios
- Current ratio (current assets / current liabilities) â€” can you pay short-term debts?
- Gross margin (gross profit / revenue) â€” efficiency of core operations
- Net margin (net income / revenue) â€” bottom line profitability
- Debt to equity â€” financial leverage and risk
- Days sales outstanding â€” how fast you collect receivables

## Budgeting
- Start with revenue projections â€” be realistic, not optimistic
- Fixed vs variable costs â€” know which expenses scale with revenue
- Compare actual vs budget monthly â€” variance analysis reveals problems
- Zero-based budgeting: justify all expenses, not just increases â€” prevents bloat
- Budget for unexpected expenses â€” something will go wrong

## Working with Accountants
- Organize documents before meetings â€” billable hours add up fast
- Ask questions until you understand â€” it's your money and liability
- Provide complete information â€” surprises during audit are expensive
- Regular check-ins, not just at tax time â€” proactive planning beats reactive filing
- Understand what they're filing on your behalf â€” you sign, you're responsible
